Protocol 03

DMSO Research

DMSO has over 11,000 peer-reviewed papers documenting its therapeutic effects. It is used in pharmaceutical research worldwide as a drug-delivery solvent. In the United States it holds exactly one FDA approval — for bladder instillation in interstitial cystitis — despite documented efficacy across dozens of conditions. The citations below represent the research base for the DMSO Protocol.

Suppression context: All DMSO clinical trials in the US were halted by the FDA in November 1965 following eye lens changes observed in dogs and rabbits at extreme doses. This effect was never observed in humans or primates. Dr. Jacob later documented that the FDA had files on over 70,000 patients from 1,500 physicians showing safety and effectiveness at the time of the halt.
01
FDA Approval for Interstitial Cystitis — RIMSO-50
FDA label · IND 1963 · Approval 1978 · ScienceDirect review 2023
RIMSO-50 (50% DMSO bladder instillation) is the only FDA-approved human use of DMSO in the United States. IND status was granted in 1963; human trials were banned in 1965; approval for this single indication came in 1978 after 13 years of regulatory limbo. The restriction to one indication despite a much wider evidence base is the central example of selective approval used throughout this guide.
ScienceDirect 2023 → FDA Label → IC Association →
02
DMSO Pharmacology — Membrane Penetration, Anti-Inflammatory, Hydroxyl Radical Scavenging
Sterling JB et al. · PubMed PMID 36459193 · 2022
Documents DMSO's core pharmacological properties: amphiphilic structure enabling membrane penetration, hydroxyl radical (·OH) scavenging, anti-inflammatory, analgesic, vasodilator, anti-platelet, radioprotective, and muscle-relaxing effects. Both DMSO and its metabolite DMS scavenge ·OH — one of the most destructive reactive oxygen species the body produces.
PubMed →
03
DMSO Toxicity Profile — LD50, Long-Term Studies, Carcinogenicity
Jacob SW & Herschler R · Cryobiology 23:14-27 · 1986 · Nature Sci Rep 2019
Comprehensive toxicity data: rat oral LD50 17.4–28.3 g/kg; 18-month rhesus monkey dermal study at 9 g/kg/day showed no intolerance; oral NOEL 3300 mg/kg/day in monkeys. DMSO is not listed as a carcinogen by IARC, NTP, OSHA, or ACGIH. For comparison, aspirin's LD50 is lower than DMSO's in most animal models.
Nature Sci Rep →
04
DMSO vs Mannitol for Traumatic Brain Injury — Intracranial Pressure Reduction
Brown FD, Johns LM, Mullan S · J Neurosurg 53(1):58-62 · 1980 · Karaca M et al. · Eur J Clin Pharmacol 40:113-114 · 1991
Two independent studies documenting DMSO's ability to reduce intracranial pressure more rapidly than mannitol — the current standard of care for traumatic brain injury. Dr. Jack de la Torre (University of Chicago) demonstrated the effect in the early 1970s. This finding has been replicated but not adopted into mainstream emergency medicine protocols.
de la Torre profile →
05
DMSO + MSM for Osteoarthritis — Systematic Review
Brien S et al. · Osteoarthritis and Cartilage · OARSI journal
Systematic review of DMSO and MSM (methylsulfonylmethane) supplementation for osteoarthritis. Both compounds reduce pain and inflammation via cell-membrane stabilisation and hydroxyl-radical scavenging. MSM is the oxidised metabolite of DMSO; they work synergistically when combined.
PubMed →
06
Topical DMSO for Anthracycline Chemotherapy Extravasation
Olver IN, Aisner J, Hament A et al. · J Clin Oncol 6(11):1732-5 · PMID 3183703 · 1988
Prospective study of 20 patients treated with topical DMSO for anthracycline extravasation (when chemotherapy leaks from a vein into surrounding tissue — a serious complication that can cause deep ulceration). None of the 20 patients progressed to ulceration. 95% confidence interval for the true ulceration rate: 1–17%.
PubMed →
07
Alpha-Synuclein Aggregation and DMSO — Parkinson's Safety Note
Reimer L, Haikal C, Gram H et al. · Scientific Reports 12:3737 · DOI 10.1038/s41598-022-07706-2 · 2022
In vitro study showing that low-concentration DMSO (<3%) induces oligomerisation and accelerates aggregation of alpha-synuclein — the protein that misfolds in Parkinson's disease. Important accuracy note: a companion experiment in the same paper found that oral DMSO (10–30%) did NOT increase alpha-synuclein aggregation in mouse brain tissue. The in vitro finding is biologically plausible enough to justify exclusion of DMSO from the Parkinson's Protocol as a precautionary measure.
Nature → PMC →
⚠️ This is the basis for DMSO's contraindication in the Parkinson's Protocol on this site. The in vitro / oral discrepancy means the picture is not fully settled — this flag reflects the precautionary position.
08
New York Academy of Sciences — DMSO Proceedings (1975)
Jacob SW & Herschler R (eds.) · Annals NY Acad Sci Vol. 243 · 1975
The primary historical source volume for DMSO clinical research. Contains the original Chilean and Argentine clinical papers: Zúñiga et al. on DMSO in infant bronchiolitis; Caro/Zúñiga Caro on DMSO for tinnitus (9 of 15 chronic cases: complete resolution); Giller et al. on cognitive effects in non-trisomy infantile developmental delay; and Dr. Lydia de Coriat's controlled study of 18 DMSO-treated children with trisomy 21 vs 91 controls — showing significant improvements in motor index, adaptation, social area, and language. These papers were published, indexed, and never followed up with US trials.

Protocol 03b

Oxygen Therapy Research

Otto Warburg received the Nobel Prize in Physiology in 1931 for demonstrating that cancer cells thrive in low-oxygen, high-glucose environments. The implications of this finding for therapeutic oxygen delivery have been studied for nearly a century — and largely excluded from mainstream oncology protocol.

01
On the Origin of Cancer Cells
Warburg O · Science 123(3191):309–314 · 1956
Warburg's landmark paper establishing that cancer cells preferentially use anaerobic fermentation (glycolysis) even in the presence of oxygen — now called the Warburg Effect. The foundational paper for the oxygen-deprivation theory of cancer. Nobel Prize in Physiology awarded 1931 for this body of work.
Science →
02
Hyperbaric Oxygen Therapy — Clinical Review
Tibbles PM, Edelsberg JS · N Engl J Med 334(25):1642–8 · 1996
New England Journal of Medicine review of HBOT's documented clinical applications — covering carbon monoxide poisoning, decompression sickness, wound healing, and radiation injury. The review establishes HBOT as a legitimate medical intervention with documented mechanisms, while noting the evidence base was limited at the time for broader applications now being studied.
PubMed →
03
Ozone: A New Medical Drug
Bocci V · Springer · 2005
The primary academic textbook on ozone therapy by Velio Bocci, Professor Emeritus of Physiology at the University of Siena. Documents ozone's mechanisms — oxidative preconditioning, cytokine modulation, antiviral and antibacterial action — and its clinical applications across infectious disease, circulatory disorders, and immune dysregulation. Bocci is the researcher who established the biological rationale for ozone therapy's therapeutic window.
Springer →
04
Ozone Therapy — Clinical Review
Elvis AM, Ekta JS · J Nat Sci Biol Med 2(1):66–70 · 2011
Peer-reviewed clinical review of ozone therapy's documented applications in dentistry, wound healing, herpes, and cardiovascular disease. Covers mechanisms including ozone's activation of antioxidant enzymes and its modulation of the immune response via cytokine pathways.
PMC →
05
Hyperbaric Oxygen Therapy for Post-Stroke Recovery — Israeli Trials
Efrati S et al. · multiple trials · Assaf Harofeh Medical Center
Series of RCTs by the Efrati group showing significant neurological recovery in post-stroke patients treated with HBOT — including patients years after the original stroke event. Mechanism: HBOT appears to reactivate dormant neurons in the ischaemic penumbra zone (the area of brain tissue that survives the stroke but is functionally inactive). This finding challenges the assumption that neurological damage from stroke is permanent.
PubMed →
06
HBOT for Traumatic Brain Injury — 2025 Meta-Analysis
Meta-analysis · 4 RCTs · 250 patients · 2025
2025 meta-analysis of four randomised controlled trials (250 patients total) confirming cognitive improvements in chronic traumatic brain injury patients treated with hyperbaric oxygen. The analysis represents the strongest current evidence base for HBOT in neurological injury outside of acute decompression sickness.

Parkinson's Protocol — Foundation

Gut-Brain Axis Research

The gut-brain connection in Parkinson's disease is among the most important areas of suppressed and under-publicised neurological research. The research below establishes the biological basis for approaching Parkinson's as a condition that may originate in the gut — not the brain.

01
Alpha-Synuclein Propagates from Gut to Brain via Vagus Nerve
Bhatt S et al. · Neuron · 2019
Landmark study demonstrating that misfolded alpha-synuclein — the protein that accumulates in Parkinson's disease — can travel from the gut to the brain via the vagus nerve in a prion-like propagation pattern. Provides the biological mechanism for Braak's staging hypothesis and establishes the gut as a potential origin point of Parkinson's pathology.
Neuron →
02
Truncal Vagotomy Reduces Parkinson's Disease Risk
Liu B et al. · Neurology · 2017 · Svensson E et al. · Annals of Neurology · 2015
Two independent epidemiological studies showing that truncal vagotomy (surgical severing of the vagus nerve, historically performed for peptic ulcer disease) significantly reduces the risk of developing Parkinson's disease. If alpha-synuclein travels from gut to brain via the vagus nerve, removing that pathway should reduce disease incidence — and it does. This is one of the strongest pieces of indirect evidence for gut-origin Parkinson's pathology.
Liu et al. PubMed → Svensson et al. PubMed →
03
SymPD Trial — Probiotic Intervention in Parkinson's Disease
Leta V et al. · Movement Disorders · DOI 10.1002/mds.70047 · 2025
First positive motor RCT of probiotic supplementation in Parkinson's disease. A 4-strain probiotic formulation produced statistically significant improvements in motor symptoms (MDS-UPDRS-III). This is the first randomised controlled trial to show that modifying the gut microbiome can produce measurable improvements in Parkinson's motor function.
Movement Disorders →
04
GUT-PARFECT Trial — Faecal Microbiota Transplant in Parkinson's Disease
Lancet eClinicalMedicine · 2024
Randomised controlled trial showing that faecal microbiota transplantation (FMT) improved MDS-UPDRS-III scores (the standard motor assessment scale) in Parkinson's patients. Published in a Lancet journal — not an alternative medicine publication. One of the first RCTs to demonstrate that transplanting a healthy microbiome into a Parkinson's patient produces measurable motor benefit.
Lancet →
05
RESISTA-PD Trial — Resistant Starch, Butyrate, and Non-Motor Symptoms
RESISTA-PD trial · published findings 2023–2024
Trial investigating resistant starch supplementation in Parkinson's disease. Resistant starch is fermented by gut bacteria into butyrate — a short-chain fatty acid with neuroprotective and anti-inflammatory properties. Results: raised butyrate levels and reduced non-motor symptoms (sleep, autonomic function, cognitive measures). Confirms the SCFA pathway as a therapeutic target in Parkinson's.

Protocol 06

Parkinson's Disease Research

The following citations cover the Braak hypothesis, environmental risk factors, and the research landscape around Parkinson's disease that does not appear in standard patient-facing materials.

01
Braak Staging — Parkinson's Pathology Begins in the Gut and Nose
Braak H et al. · Neurobiology of Aging · 2003
Heiko Braak's landmark staging system showing that Parkinson's pathology follows a predictable anatomical progression — beginning in the enteric nervous system (gut) and olfactory bulb (nose), and travelling up to the brain only in later stages. Stage 1–2 pathology is exclusively peripheral. This staging system, now named after Braak, provides the anatomical framework for gut-first Parkinson's theories.
PubMed →
02
Depleted Bacterial Strains in Parkinson's Disease Patients
Multiple studies · 2019–2023 · Cell, Nature Medicine, Movement Disorders
Multiple independent studies across different populations consistently find the same bacterial strains depleted in Parkinson's patients compared to healthy controls: Faecalibacterium prausnitzii, Roseburia intestinalis, Blautia, Anaerostipes hadrus, Eubacterium rectale, and Coprococcus. These are all butyrate-producing bacteria. Their depletion reduces the gut's production of neuroprotective short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) and indole-3-propionic acid (IPA).
03
Soursop / Graviola and Parkinson's Disease Risk — Critical Warning
Champy P et al. · Movement Disorders · 2004 · Lannuzel A et al. · 2008
Critical finding for anyone following the Dr. Sebi Alkaline Protocol: Annona species (soursop, graviola, custard apple) contain annonacin — a mitochondrial complex I inhibitor with the same mechanism as MPTP, the compound used to chemically induce Parkinson's in research animals. Two peer-reviewed studies by Champy (2004) and Lannuzel (2008) document a significant association between soursop consumption and an atypical Parkinson's-like syndrome in Guadeloupe. Soursop is explicitly contraindicated for anyone with Parkinson's or at elevated risk.
Champy 2004 → Lannuzel 2008 →
⚠️ This is a safety-critical citation. Soursop is featured in the Dr. Sebi Alkaline Protocol and is contraindicated for Parkinson's disease. Both protocols carry this warning explicitly.

Glyphosate Research

Glyphosate is the most widely used herbicide in the world. The research below documents its neurological effects, the industry suppression of that research, and the dietary intervention data showing how quickly it can be cleared from the body.

01
Paraquat, Parkinson's Disease, and the Role of Agnotology
Dorsey ER et al. · Movement Disorders · DOI 10.1002/mds.29371 · 2023
Published in Movement Disorders — the field's leading journal — this paper names Syngenta and its lobbying firm by name and documents the systematic manufacture of doubt around paraquat's link to Parkinson's disease. Uses the term "agnotology" (the study of culturally manufactured ignorance) to describe the strategy. April 2025: Syngenta settled approximately 6,500 paraquat lawsuits. March 2026: Syngenta announced the end of global paraquat production by June 2026.
Movement Disorders →
02
One Week of Organic Diet Reduces Urinary Glyphosate by 70.93%
Fagan J et al. · Environmental Research · 2020
Controlled dietary intervention showing that switching to an organic diet for one week reduces urinary glyphosate levels by an average of 70.93%. This is the foundational evidence for the dietary component of the glyphosate binder protocol — the body clears glyphosate rapidly when dietary exposure is removed.
PubMed →
03
Glyphosate Levels in Common Foods — Independent Testing
The Detox Project · 2022 · Independent laboratory testing
Independent testing of commercially available foods for glyphosate residues. Highest levels found: Cheerios Oat Crunch Cinnamon (1,171 ppb), Village Hearth 100% Whole Wheat Bread (1,150 ppb), 365 Whole Wheat Sandwich Bread (1,040 ppb), Great Value Chickpeas (889 ppb), Honey Nut Cheerios (833–894 ppb), Pepperidge Farm 100% Whole Grain Wheat (587 ppb), Quaker Oats (535 ppb). Products marketed as "whole grain" and "natural" are consistently among the highest-residue items tested.

Minerals & Deficiencies Research

The following citations support the Hidden Deficiencies Protocol — covering iodine, magnesium, zinc, copper, and their interactions. Mineral deficiency is one of the most under-diagnosed contributors to chronic disease in modern populations.

01
Iodine Deficiency as the World's Most Preventable Cause of Intellectual Disability
WHO / UNICEF / ICCIDD · multiple publications · 1993–2022
WHO documentation establishing iodine deficiency as the world's most common preventable cause of intellectual disability and the leading preventable cause of hypothyroidism. The halogen displacement mechanism — fluoride, bromide, and chloride competing with iodine for the same cellular receptors — is established biochemistry underlying the modern iodine deficiency epidemic despite widespread iodised salt use.
WHO →
02
Zinc-Copper Interaction — Copper Depletion from Zinc Supplementation
Multiple peer-reviewed sources · established clinical pharmacology
Zinc and copper compete for the same intestinal absorption transporters (metallothionein). Unsupplemented zinc use at therapeutic doses depletes copper within 4–6 weeks. The zinc-copper ratio is a known clinical consideration — high-dose zinc supplementation without copper monitoring is associated with copper deficiency anaemia and neurological symptoms. Critical rule from this guide: never take zinc and copper simultaneously — minimum 2 hours apart.
PubMed — original zinc-copper interaction →
03
Magnesium Deficiency — Prevalence and Health Consequences
DiNicolantonio JJ et al. · Open Heart · 2018
Review documenting that over 50% of people in developed countries consume less than the recommended daily amount of magnesium, and that subclinical magnesium deficiency is a principal driver of cardiovascular disease, insulin resistance, and neurological dysfunction. Magnesium is a cofactor in over 300 enzymatic reactions — its deficiency affects virtually every system in the body.
PMC →
