Part One

The Brain's Actual Needs

The brain is 60% fat. It runs on glucose — or, when properly conditioned, ketones. It consumes 20% of the body's total oxygen supply. It is protected by the most selective barrier in biology — the blood-brain barrier — and it is simultaneously the most vulnerable organ to the substances that can breach it.

Everything that reaches the brain does so either because the body designed it to cross, or because it shares enough chemical properties with something the body designed to cross that it slips through. Understanding this one fact changes everything about your neurological environment.

"The blood-brain barrier is not a wall. It is a selective gate. The tragedy is not that it fails completely — it is that it was never designed for the chemical environment of the 21st century."

What the Brain Runs On — And What It's Being Denied

Fat: The myelin sheath around every nerve fiber is approximately 70% fat. When dietary fat is chronically low or the wrong type, myelin integrity degrades — producing cognitive slowing, mood instability, and neurological symptoms that look like a dozen different diseases.

Cholesterol: 25% of the body's total cholesterol is in the brain. Statins suppress cholesterol synthesis throughout the body including the brain. The documented cognitive side effects — memory loss, confusion, personality changes — are direct consequences. They appear in published research. They are largely ignored in clinical practice.

Oxygen: The brain uses 20% of the body's oxygen despite being 2% of body weight. Even subtle reductions in cerebral oxygenation produce measurable cognitive impairment. This is why the DMSO & Oxygen protocol is part of this stack.

Minerals: Magnesium is required for every nerve firing. Zinc governs synaptic plasticity. Copper is required for myelin synthesis. Iodine governs thyroid hormones that regulate myelination throughout life. All four are commonly deficient. None appear on a standard neurological workup.

Part Two — The Hidden Source

Aluminum, Your Teeth, and Your Brain

Professor Christopher Exley of Keele University spent 30 years researching aluminum biology. His findings are unambiguous: aluminum accumulates in the brain, co-localizes with the protein deposits found in neurodegenerative brain tissue, and accumulates at significantly higher levels in people with neurological diseases than in healthy controls. There is no safe level of aluminum in brain tissue — because it is not supposed to be there at all.

"The unequivocal neurotoxicity of aluminum means that when brain burdens exceed toxic thresholds, it is inevitable that aluminum contributes to disease. The question is not whether aluminum is neurotoxic. That is settled."
— Professor Christopher Exley, Keele University, 2017

The Silica Protocol — Removing Aluminum from the Brain

Silicic acid forms a stable complex with aluminum ions and facilitates their excretion through urine — including aluminum accumulated in brain tissue. Published in peer-reviewed literature with human clinical data. In Exley's study, 8 of 15 patients with progressive neurological decline showed no further deterioration and 3 showed measurable cognitive improvement after high-silica water intervention.

Standard histological staining does not detect aluminum. For decades researchers could claim no aluminum was present — because the techniques used couldn't find it. Exley's group developed aluminum-specific fluorescence microscopy using lumogallion, and looked properly for the first time. What they found: aluminum throughout the brain tissue of people who died with neurodegenerative disease, co-located with the protein deposits that characterize these conditions. Every disease group showed higher brain aluminum than healthy controls.

A 1991 study (Hirsch et al., Journal of Neurochemistry) measured aluminum and iron in the substantia nigra — the brain region that degenerates in movement disorders like Parkinson's — and found both significantly elevated compared to controls. The mechanism: aluminum disrupts iron metabolism via ceruloplasmin impairment, and iron accumulation in the substantia nigra is now an accepted pathological finding. This aluminum → iron dysregulation → substantia nigra toxicity pathway is documented and mechanistically coherent. It is not discussed in standard neurology.

Neurogenesis — Growing New Brain

Adult neurogenesis — the birth of new neurons in the adult brain — is documented, real, and responsive to specific inputs. The molecule that governs it is Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor (BDNF) — essentially a growth hormone for brain cells. Every lifestyle intervention that reliably improves brain function does so, in part, by growing it.

"The brain is not a fixed structure. It is a living, dynamic, continuously remodelling organ that responds — within hours — to what you do, what you think, and what you put into your body. This is not optimism. This is neuroscience."

The Missing Chapter

The Gut-Brain Axis — Where It May Have Begun

In 2003, German neuroanatomist Heiko Braak published a staging hypothesis that quietly changed everything — and then was quietly ignored. His finding: the misfolded protein at the center of Parkinson's and movement disorder decline appears first in the gut wall neurons and the olfactory system. It travels to the brain via the vagus nerve. By the time any motor symptom appears, the process has been underway for years — sometimes a decade or more.

Two large registry studies confirmed a striking finding: people who had undergone truncal vagotomy — surgical severing of the vagus nerve — showed significantly reduced risk of developing a movement disorder in the decades that followed. Liu et al. (Neurology, 2017) using the Swedish registry. Svensson et al. (Annals of Neurology, 2015) using the Danish registry. Cut the highway — and the disease does not arrive.

"The brain did not start the disease. The research suggests the gut did. That changes what intervention means."

Six bacterial strains are consistently depleted in people with movement disorders: Faecalibacterium prausnitzii, Roseburia intestinalis, Blautia, Anaerostipes hadrus, Eubacterium rectale, and Coprococcus. These are butyrate producers. Butyrate feeds the gut lining, dampens neuroinflammation, and governs vagal signalling the brain depends on.

And then there is glyphosate. E. Ray Dorsey's 2023 paper in Movement Disorders (doi: 10.1002/mds.29371) names the lobbying firm, names the corporation, and documents how a leading EPA scientific advisor was removed from a review panel after her research connected pesticide exposure to neurological movement disorders. Syngenta entered settlement agreements for ~6,500 related lawsuits in April 2025 and announced the end of global paraquat production by June 2026.
